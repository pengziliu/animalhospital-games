# CCTV Anomalies – Camera Monitor Guide

CCTV anomalies are **only visible on the security camera monitors** in your office. They cannot be seen by looking at the patient in person or on the polaroid photo. You must actively watch the camera feeds — particularly the **waiting room camera**, which is the most important monitor.

The CCTV check is the **third and final step** of the anomaly screening protocol.

---

## Why the Waiting Room Camera Matters

The waiting room camera is your **primary surveillance feed**. Most CCTV anomalies manifest on this camera because it covers the area where patients sit before being called in. Check it frequently — a quick glance between patient visits is often enough to catch anomalies.

## Detailed Breakdown

### 1. Censored / JPEG Eyes

The patient's eyes on the camera feed appear **pixelated, blurred, or covered by a censor block** — like a redacted document or a heavily compressed JPEG.

- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Detection tip:** The censor block is immediately obvious. If the patient's eye area looks blurred or pixelated while the rest of the image is clear, it's an anomaly.
- **Common mistake:** Confusing normal CCTV compression with this anomaly. Censored Eyes are localized to the eye area only.

### 2. Unnatural Body / Limb Stretching

The patient's body on camera appears **distorted** — limbs may be stretched too long, bent at impossible angles, or the overall body proportions look wrong.

- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Detection tip:** If the patient's silhouette looks wrong — too long, too thin, or with limbs going the wrong direction — it's this anomaly.

### 3. Hollow Face (CCTV)

The patient on camera has **empty black holes for eyes**, similar to the in-person Hollow Face anomaly but viewed through the grainy CCTV feed.

- **Difficulty:** Hard
- **Detection tip:** This is harder to spot on CCTV than in person due to lower image quality. Look carefully at the patient's face on the monitor — if you see dark voids where eyes should be, it's an anomaly.

### 4. Staring At Camera

The patient on the CCTV feed is **looking directly at the camera**, maintaining unbroken eye contact with the lens. Normal patients do not look at security cameras.

- **Difficulty:** Medium
- **Detection tip:** If you feel like the patient on screen is staring back at you, they probably are. This one has an instinctive "wrongness" to it.

### 5. Void Bodies / Shadow Patient

The camera shows a **completely dark silhouette** or shadowy figure instead of a normal patient. The figure may be entirely black, semi-transparent, or made of shadow.

- **Difficulty:** Easy
- **Detection tip:** A dark, shadow-like form sitting where a patient should be is immediately suspicious. No normal patient appears as a silhouette.

### 6. Skinwalker (CCTV)

The patient on the CCTV feed moves **erratically or mimics unnatural behavior** — sudden movements, jerky motions, or behavior that doesn't match a normal waiting patient.

- **Difficulty:** Medium
- **Detection tip:** Watch for movement that seems "off." A Skinwalker on CCTV may shift position suddenly, move in ways a normal patient wouldn't, or display uncanny animations.

### 7. Mismatched Ears/Eyes (CCTV)

The patient's ears or eyes on the CCTV feed **do not match what you observed during the in-person appearance check**.

- **Difficulty:** Very Hard
- **Detection tip:** This is the hardest CCTV anomaly to detect because it requires you to remember specific details from the in-person inspection and compare them against the low-quality camera feed. Take notes mentally during the appearance check.
- **Common mistake:** Skipping the in-person check, making it impossible to detect this anomaly.

### 8. Twitching (CCTV)

The patient's limbs **jerk and spasm on the camera feed**, similar to the in-person Twitching anomaly but viewed through CCTV.

- **Difficulty:** Hard
- **Detection tip:** CCTV footage is grainy, so subtle twitching can be hard to see. Watch the patient's limbs for any rhythmic or irregular movement over a few seconds.

## Detection Strategy

### Recommended Workflow

1. **Complete the in-person appearance check** (Step 1 of screening protocol).
2. **Inspect the polaroid photo** (Step 2 of screening protocol).
3. **Check the CCTV monitors**, starting with the **waiting room camera**.
4. **Scan for Easy anomalies first** — Censored Eyes, Unnatural Body, Void Bodies.
5. **Watch each patient for a few seconds** — look for Staring At Camera, Skinwalker movement, Twitching.
6. **Compare CCTV appearance to your in-person memory** — check for Mismatched features and Hollow Face.
7. **If anything is wrong on any camera, close the shutter.**

### Pro Tips

- **Make CCTV checking a habit.** Glance at the waiting room camera between every patient interaction.
- **Don't rush the Very Hard anomaly.** Mismatched Ears/Eyes on CCTV requires deliberate comparison — it's okay to take a few extra seconds.
- **Low resolution is your enemy.** If you're unsure about something on CCTV, lean on the side of caution and close the shutter.
